## Important: these are not types

All three modes exist in every person. All three are necessary:

- **Mobilization** helps you act in real danger, provides energy for sports, competition, urgent tasks
- **Freeze** protects from overload, helps "shut down" when things are too much
- **Connection** enables relationships, learning, cooperation, rest

The question isn't "what type are you," but:

1. **Which mode do you spend the most time in?**
2. **How quickly can you switch between them?**
3. **Where do you get stuck?**

## Patterns

Every person has their own pattern â€” a habitual "territory" on the map of three modes. Here are some common ones:

**"Pendulum"** â€” swinging between mobilization and freeze. Wired, then shut down. Connection mode is barely accessible. A very exhausting pattern.

**"Perpetual motion"** â€” near-constant mobilization. Always busy, can't stop. Others may see them as "energetic," but inside â€” constant tension. Freeze comes only as total collapse (burnout).

**"Behind glass"** â€” predominantly freeze. The world feels muted, distant. The person "functions" but on autopilot. May appear calm, but it's not calm â€” it's disconnection.

**"Sensitive"** â€” connection mode is available but easily disrupted. A small stressor pushes them into mobilization or freeze. Rapid switching, high reactivity.

**"Resilient"** â€” predominantly connection mode with good ability to return to it after stress. This isn't the "ideal" â€” it's one pattern among others, with its own limitations (for example, such a person may underestimate real threats).

## What shapes your pattern

Your autonomic pattern is not a life sentence or a "personality." It's shaped by:

- **Early experience** â€” relationships with caregivers in the first years of life literally shape the nervous system's settings
- **Accumulated stress** â€” chronic stress shifts the pattern toward mobilization or freeze
- **Current environment** â€” noisy office, conflictual relationships, financial pressure â€” all of this matters
- **Physical health** â€” sleep, nutrition, movement directly affect autonomic regulation
- **Practice** â€” the pattern can be consciously changed. Slowly, but for real

### Stephen Porges' Polyvagal Theory

The three-mode model described above is based on the Polyvagal Theory, developed by neurophysiologist Stephen Porges in 1994 and refined since.

The key insight: the vagus nerve (nervus vagus) â€” the longest cranial nerve, connecting the brain to the heart, lungs, gut, and other organs â€” consists of two branches, not one, as previously assumed.

**Dorsal branch (dorsal vagal complex)**
- Evolutionarily ancient, shared by all vertebrates
- Unmyelinated (slow signal transmission)
- Responsible for "freeze": reduced heart rate, blood pressure, metabolism
- When overactivated â€” fainting, dissociation, shutdown

**Ventral branch (ventral vagal complex)**
- Evolutionarily recent, developed in mammals
- Myelinated (fast signal transmission)
- Connected to muscles of the face, middle ear, larynx, pharynx
- Responsible for "social engagement": voice prosody, facial expression, ability to distinguish human speech from background noise
- Its tone is measured via heart rate variability (HRV) â€” specifically respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA)

**Response hierarchy (neuroception)**

Porges proposed the concept of "neuroception" â€” the unconscious assessment of environmental safety. Depending on the result, the nervous system activates one of three modes in a specific order:

1. If the environment is safe â†’ ventral vagus â†’ connection mode
2. If there's a threat â†’ sympathetic system â†’ mobilization
3. If the threat is overwhelming â†’ dorsal vagus â†’ freeze

This order â€” from newest to oldest â€” reflects evolutionary history. The system "tries" the most complex and adaptive response first, and if it fails â€” falls back to a more primitive one.

### Criticism and limitations

Polyvagal Theory is not uncontested in the scientific community. Key criticisms:

- Some neuroanatomical claims are disputed (particularly the degree of separation between dorsal and ventral branches)
- Clinical applications of the theory sometimes go beyond what is strictly supported experimentally
- The concept of "neuroception" is difficult to operationalize for experimental verification

Nevertheless, the basic claims â€” the existence of two vagal branches, the link between vagal tone and emotional regulation, the role of the autonomic nervous system in social behavior â€” have strong experimental support.

In InnerWire, we use Polyvagal Theory as a useful framework for understanding autonomic states, but not as dogma. The model will be updated as research evolves.
