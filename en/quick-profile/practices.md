# Quick Profile: Starter Practices

You don't need to do everything. Pick 1–2 practices that match what you noticed in the [questionnaire](questionnaire.md). Try them for one week before adding more.

The principle behind all of these: **notice first, change second.** Most people try to fix things they haven't actually observed. Observation itself is the first practice.

---

## Nervous system

**For everyone: Mode check-in**

Three times a day — morning, afternoon, evening — pause for 30 seconds. Ask yourself: *"Am I in connection, mobilization, or freeze right now?"* Don't try to change anything. Just notice. One word is enough.

Why: Noticing your state activates the part of the nervous system that supports calm engagement. Observation itself shifts you slightly toward connection.

**If you're mostly on edge:** Extend your exhale. Breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6–8. The exhale activates the vagal brake — the mechanism that slows your system down. Two minutes, a few times a day, especially before sleep.

**If you're mostly shut down:** Gentle movement with rhythm. Walking, stretching, rocking — anything that adds a little activation without overwhelming. Your system needs a small push toward mobilization before it can reach connection.

## Body sensing

**For everyone: 60-second body scan**

Three times a day, scan through your body: feet, legs, belly, chest, hands, shoulders, neck, face. For each area — is there a sensation? Tension, warmth, heaviness, nothing? Don't change anything. Just notice. End with: *"On a scale from pleasant to unpleasant — how does my body feel right now?"*

Why: Brief, regular scans train the neural pathways between body and brain. Frequency matters more than duration.

**If you pay a lot of attention but aren't sure it's accurate:** Before interpreting a body sensation ("this means I'm anxious"), try staying with the raw sensation for a few seconds. Warm? Tight? Fluttery? Label the sensation, not the emotion.

**If your body is mostly quiet:** Start with the easiest signals — notice warmth in your hands after holding a hot cup, or the feeling of your feet on the ground. Build from what's already noticeable.

## Sensory processing

**For everyone: Sensory diary (1 week)**

Three times a day, take 60 seconds and note: What am I noticing right now? What's bothering me? What's missing? How's my energy?

Why: Most people don't connect their energy and mood to the sensory environment they're in. One week of logging reveals patterns you didn't know you had.

**If you're sensitive:** Identify your top 2–3 triggers (noise, light, crowds) and build in recovery time after exposure. Noise-canceling headphones, a dim room, 15 minutes of quiet — not as a luxury, but as maintenance.

**If you need more stimulation:** Notice when you're understimulated (restless, bored, doing risky things for the thrill) and give yourself input deliberately — music, movement, cold water, varied textures — before you seek it impulsively.

## Closeness with others

**For everyone: Relationship moment reflection**

Once a day, at the end of the day, recall one interaction with someone close. Ask: What happened? What did I feel in my body? What did I do — move closer, pull away, seek reassurance, shut down? No judgment, just notice the pattern.

Why: Attachment patterns operate automatically. The first step to having more choice is seeing what happens before you choose.

**If you tend toward anxiety in closeness:** When you feel the urge to check in, seek reassurance, or test someone — pause and notice the body sensation underneath. Tightness? Hollowness? Stay with the sensation for 30 seconds before acting. This isn't about suppressing the impulse, but about creating a gap between feeling and response.

**If you tend toward avoidance:** Notice when you pull away — not to stop yourself, but to see what triggered it. Was it vulnerability? Dependency? Someone needing you? Just register the moment and the body sensation.

## Stress load

**If your load is moderate or higher: Stress audit**

Take 20 minutes. List every recurring demand on your system: work, relationships, finances, health, environment. Then mark each: (a) I can reduce this, (b) I can't reduce it but I can recover from it better, (c) I can't do anything about it right now. Focus your energy on (a) and (b).

Why: You can't lower allostatic load by adding recovery practices alone if the incoming load is unsustainable. Identify what's filling the bucket before trying to empty it.

**For everyone: Protect one recovery window**

Pick one recovery activity that actually restores you (not scrolling, not alcohol — something after which you feel genuinely better). Protect 30 minutes for it, 3+ times a week. Treat it as non-negotiable.

Why: Recovery isn't passive — it's an active process that requires time and the right conditions. Most people lose their recovery windows first when life gets busy, which accelerates the load.

## Starting small

If five axes feel like too much — pick the one section from the questionnaire where you had the strongest reaction ("that's me"). Start there. One practice, one week. Then see what you notice.

The point isn't to optimize your profile. It's to understand how you work — and have more choice in what you do with that.
